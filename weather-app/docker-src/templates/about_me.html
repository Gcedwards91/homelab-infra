George "Cliff" Edwards

This page is a live cover letter. It is the story of this project, my story as a man, and as an engineer.

In The Beginning...

I initially started in automotive collision repair before pivoting to food service for years, and eventually taught myself how to build a gaming PC. I saved tip money from three years at Outback Steakhouse, bought PC parts, built my own machine, and researched my way into an IT job at a POS reseller in 2016 — I was incredibly inexperienced, my most technical feat to date was building my own PC. That said, my manager at this reseller took a gamble on me. I am thankful he did.

Within six months I was doing project management alongside helpdesk work. Within eighteen months I was recruited by Arby's Restaurant Group as a contractor to help roll out a new POS system — the same POS system I supported in my role at the time — across their entire corporate install base. This would be my first real taste of enterprise engineering.

The Arby's Years — Becoming a Platform Engineer

I joined Arby's Initially hired on as a contractor for three specific purposes: to train the Arby's Service Desk on supporting Brink POS at scale, to assist the POS DBA team in configuration management via Brink POS's Settings Editor, and to assist the Arby's Mobile Project in the early development and pilot of Arby's Mobile Ordering App. and then Arby's turned into Inspire Brands. The parent company of Arby's, Buffalo Wild Wings, Sonic, Rusty Taco, Jimmy John's, Dunkin Donuts, and Baskin Robbins. I left before the Dunkin acquisition. While my contract was 8 months (January through August 2018), I was offered a full time position in early June as an Engineer on the Field Technology Services team. In this capacity, I functioned as the in-house PAR/Brink POS expert and was responsible for maintaining the health of the environment with that in mind. Eventually, I began functioning as a Platform Engineer Sole infrastructure engineer over an 18,000 endpoint POS environment across 1,200+ corporate locations. Policy configuration and enforcement was a challenge as I was not given a domain controller. Policy was enforced by a cron job on LANDesk that used PowerShell to invoke LGPO.exe to import a policy export. and inherited the rest of the storeside environment. It was an environment beleaguered by technical debt. Foundational tooling such as WSUS, WDS, and a domain controller were not available. Alternatives had to be built using PowerShell and LANDesk.

I was the sole engineer responsible for the stability of an 18,000 endpoint POS environment across 1,200+ corporate locations. The devices I managed differed in function and included POS Registers, Kitchen Video Devices, Receipt Printers, Cash Drawers, Order Confirmation Boards, and Manager's Workstations. I built the Windows imaging pipeline from scratch using MDT, replacing a brittle sector-by-sector DISM capture process that was propagating hardware defects across the install base with a layered imaging process. I reverse-engineered all existing deployment automation — without source code — and rewrote it in PowerShell with redundancy, error handling, and logging. I built a suite of GUI-driven configuration tools used by field technicians and service desk agents across the entire install base. I built a snapshot capture and revert system that eliminated the need to reimage on-site in the majority of cases.

When I arrived, aggregate system uptime across the install base was approximately 85%. When I left, it was 94%. The last bit came down to above-store (i.e., cloud) integrations and local store instability.

The Pivot

After Arby's I spent four years in technical support — first at Tripwire Supported containerized environments with Rancher and Kubernetes. Built PowerShell lab-breaking tools to simulate customer environments for engineer training. , then at Tenable Sr. Technical Support Engineer handling high-priority enterprise escalations involving Docker, Kubernetes, and SIEM integrations. KCS coach. Escalations lead. . Both roles gave me exposure to Docker and Kubernetes in real enterprise environments — actual production deployments that were breaking and needed diagnosing. They also assisted in rounding out my skillset. As a self-taught engineer, my skills were centered around demand. The scope of demand widened in these roles, my skillset followed. I saw how infrastructure should be built by watching what happened when it wasn't.

I decided to stop supporting that layer and start owning it. While being a Support Engineer can be both challenging and rewarding, I missed systems engineering greatly. This project is the culmination of my goal to move back to systems engineering via DevOps or SRE.

This Project

What you're looking at right now is a containerized, fully instrumented application stack — a Flask web application served by Gunicorn behind an nginx reverse proxy, with Prometheus metrics, Loki log aggregation, Promtail autodiscovery, and Grafana dashboards provisioned as code.

The Grafana dashboard Powered by Statporter — a custom Prometheus exporter written from scratch in Python. Collects per-container CPU, memory, network, and disk I/O metrics by querying the Docker socket directly. Built to work around cgroups v2 compatibility issues with existing exporters. is powered by a custom Prometheus exporter I wrote called Statporter. I built it because existing exporters had incomplete cgroups v2 support at the time. Rather than wait for someone else to fix it, I built my own.

Every service has a healthcheck. Every image is pinned to a specific version. Credentials are managed via environment variables and excluded from version control. The entire stack deploys from a single docker compose up.

What's Next

The next phase is a Kubernetes migration and AWS deployment — ALB for load balancing, ACM for TLS, Route53 for DNS, Secrets Manager replacing the local environment file, and all of it wrapped in Terraform. The AWS Solutions Architect Associate certification is in progress. The CKA follows.

The goal is a fully production-grade, publicly accessible deployment of everything you're looking at now. Infrastructure as code, end to end, with a real domain and real TLS — built and owned by one person.

Self Discovery

This project is, in a lot of ways, inspired by the Cloud Resume Challenge. Building it forced me to learn a lot — not only technology or problem-solving, it activated some old, lost muscle memory. It forced me to relive my career in a way bullet points on a resume never could. To take stock of my journey. The result of that is some self discovery. I am self-taught. I cannot say I am not naturally curious about this industry, but I learn best by demand, not through academic curiosity. Whatever the problem in front of me needs me to learn to overcome it, I learn it. This, of course, leaves me with gaps. I will be honest about that. Even still, building this page, my dynamic resume page, and this project reminded me of one thing. Then reinforced it. No matter the gaps I have, I have a proven track record of high performance under pressure, stress, and constraints. I will show up, and whatever I touch, it improves.

I do not intend to sound arrogant or defensive. I intend to sound pragmatic. For any recruiters reading this, if I am not "the guy", I am not "the guy". Simple as that, and I accept it. Simultaneously, if you are the betting type, the people who have bet on me in their careers have all benefitted greatly from it. I have as well. I have learned what I learned the hard way. Not through academia, not through theory alone. Through time in the trenches.
